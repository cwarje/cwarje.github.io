Main objective

The winner is the first player to reach 10 victory points during their turn.

You usually get victory points from:

settlements: 1 point each
cities: 2 points each
Longest Road: 2 points
Largest Army: 2 points
some development cards: 1 point each if they are victory point cards
Components in the base game

The exact physical appearance varies a bit by edition, but the base game includes these core components:

Board pieces
Terrain hexes

These make the island. Each hex represents one terrain type that produces one kind of resource.

The standard productive terrain types are:

forest → produces lumber
hills → produces brick
pasture → produces wool
fields → produces grain
mountains → produces ore

There is also:

desert → produces nothing and starts with the robber on it
Number tokens

These round tokens are placed on most terrain hexes. They show numbers from 2 to 12 except 7.

When that number is rolled, the corresponding hex produces resources for players with buildings touching it.

The more common numbers statistically are more valuable:

6 and 8 are strongest
then 5 and 9
then 4 and 10
then 3 and 11
then 2 and 12 are weakest
Sea frame / outer border

The island is surrounded by ocean.

Ports / harbors

Some coastal intersections connect to ports that improve maritime trade.

Types:

3:1 port: trade any 3 identical resources for any 1 resource
2:1 specialized port: trade 2 of a specific resource for any 1 resource

You only gain use of a port if you have a settlement or city on the correct coastal intersection touching that port.

Player pieces

Each player has a color and their own set of:

Roads

Used to connect your network across the board.

Settlements

Small houses. These:

are worth 1 victory point
collect resources from adjacent terrain
can be upgraded into cities
must follow spacing rules
Cities

Larger upgraded buildings. These:

are worth 2 victory points
collect double resources from adjacent terrain
replace settlements
Cards
Resource cards

There are 5 resource types:

brick
lumber
wool
grain
ore

These are the main currency of the game. You spend them to build and buy development cards.

Development cards

These are bought from a deck and kept hidden until used or revealed when appropriate.

Types in the base game:

Knight
Road Building
Year of Plenty
Monopoly
Victory Point

These add hidden information, surprise effects, and alternative paths to points or control.

Other pieces
Robber

A special piece that begins on the desert. It affects production and stealing.

Dice

Two six-sided dice.

Longest Road card

Given to the player who currently holds the longest qualifying road network.

Largest Army card

Given to the player who has played the most knights, once they qualify.

Board setup
Standard setup

In a standard game, the terrain hexes are arranged into the classic Settlers island shape. Number tokens are placed on them in a prescribed pattern if using fixed beginner setup, or according to setup rules if randomizing.

In many groups, people either use:

beginner preset setup for learning
random setup for normal play
Desert

The desert gets no number token and starts with the robber on it.

Ports

Ports go around the outside edge in set positions according to the frame.

Basic map structure: hexes, intersections, and edges

This is essential to understand.

Hexes

The terrain spaces where resources come from.

Intersections

The corners where hexes meet. This is where you place:

settlements
cities
Edges

The lines between intersections. This is where you place:

roads

So:

buildings go on intersections
roads go on edges
resources come from hexes
How resource production works

Each productive hex has:

a terrain type
a number token

If the dice roll matches that number, the hex produces.

Example

Suppose a forest hex has the number 8. Whenever an 8 is rolled:

every settlement touching that forest gets 1 lumber
every city touching that forest gets 2 lumber

If multiple players have buildings touching that hex, all of them get resources.

If a player’s building touches several hexes that produce on a roll, they collect from each of them.

Important rule

If the robber is on a hex, that hex produces nothing, even if its number is rolled.

Starting the game

Before the main turn sequence begins, players go through initial placement.

Initial placement order

Players place starting pieces in a snake order:

Player 1
Player 2
Player 3
Player 4
then reverse:
Player 4
Player 3
Player 2
Player 1

For each placement, the player places:

1 settlement
1 connected road

So by the end of setup, each player has:

2 settlements
2 roads
Initial resource collection

After everyone places both starting settlements, each player collects resources from the hexes adjacent to their second settlement only.

If that settlement touches:

forest
pasture
fields

they would take:

1 lumber
1 wool
1 grain

They do not collect from desert.

This gives players their starting hand for the main game.

Settlement placement rules

Settlements are heavily restricted.

Distance rule

A settlement can only be built at an intersection if no settlement or city is on any adjacent intersection.

In simpler terms:

there must always be at least one empty intersection space between settlements/cities

People often call this the distance rule or “two roads apart,” though that wording can oversimplify it.

This rule applies:

during setup
during normal building later
Connection rule during the game

After setup, any new settlement you build must be:

connected to your own road network
and must still obey the distance rule

You cannot just drop a settlement anywhere legal. It must connect through your roads.

Road placement rules

A road must generally be placed on an edge connected to:

one of your existing roads
or one of your own settlements/cities

You cannot place disconnected roads elsewhere on the map.

Also, another player’s building can interrupt your road path for expansion purposes.

City rules

A city is built by upgrading one of your own settlements.

You do not place a city on an empty intersection. You replace an existing settlement.

Effects:

the settlement is removed and returned to your supply
the city is placed there instead
that location is now worth 2 victory points instead of 1
it produces 2 resources instead of 1 whenever adjacent hexes activate
Structure of a turn

A normal turn in Settlers has three main parts:

Roll for production
Trade
Build

These are often thought of as phases, though the game is fairly flexible after the roll.

Phase 1: Roll the dice

At the start of your turn, you roll two dice.

If the result is 2–6 or 8–12

All hexes showing that number produce resources.

Every player with a settlement or city adjacent to those hexes receives resources.

This means all players can benefit from a roll, not just the active player.

If the result is 7

The robber activates instead of normal production.

That triggers special rules.

The 7 and the robber

Rolling a 7 is a major disruptive event.

Step 1: Hand limit discard

Any player with more than 7 resource cards in hand must discard half of them, rounded down.

Examples:

8 cards → discard 4
9 cards → discard 4
10 cards → discard 5
11 cards → discard 5

Players with 7 or fewer discard nothing.

Step 2: Move the robber

The active player moves the robber to any hex on the board, including a different player’s productive hex or even the desert.

Step 3: Steal one random resource

If one or more opponents have a settlement or city adjacent to the robber’s new hex, the active player chooses one of those opponents and steals 1 random resource card from them.

If no eligible opponent is adjacent, no card is stolen.

Ongoing effect

As long as the robber sits on a hex:

that hex produces nothing
Phase 2: Trading

After rolling, the active player may trade.

There are two main types.

Domestic trade

This means trading with other players.

You can offer:

specific resources
package deals
conditional negotiation in table-talk terms, depending on the group

But mechanically, trades are just immediate exchanges of cards.

Example:

“I’ll give 2 wool for 1 ore.”

Only the active player may initiate and complete trades with others during their turn. Other players cannot trade among themselves when it is not their turn.

Maritime trade

This means trading with the bank.

Standard bank trade

Without a port, the default rate is:

4 of the same resource for 1 of any resource

Example:

4 wool for 1 ore
3:1 port

If you have access to a 3:1 port:

3 of the same resource for 1 of any resource
2:1 port

If you have access to a specific 2:1 port:

2 of that named resource for 1 of any resource

Example:
If you have a brick port:

2 brick for 1 of any resource
Phase 3: Building and buying

After or between trades, you can spend resources to build. In practice, players often trade and build in whatever order they like after the roll, as long as everything is legal.

Building costs
Road

Cost:

1 brick
1 lumber

Purpose:

expand network
reach good settlement spots
compete for Longest Road
block opponents indirectly by claiming paths first
Settlement

Cost:

1 brick
1 lumber
1 wool
1 grain

Purpose:

gain 1 victory point
increase production reach
gain access to new ports

Restrictions:

must connect to your road
must obey distance rule
City

Cost:

3 ore
2 grain

Purpose:

upgrade production
increase point value from 1 to 2 at that spot
Development card

Cost:

1 ore
1 wool
1 grain

Purpose:

gain hidden tactical or scoring advantages
Development cards in detail

When you buy a development card:

draw the top card from the deck
keep it hidden from opponents
you usually cannot play it on the same turn you bought it

The key exception in effect is that victory point cards simply sit hidden and count when needed; they are not “played” as an action card.

Knight

Effect:

move the robber as though a 7 was rolled
steal 1 random card from an eligible adjacent opponent

Important:

playing knights is how you compete for Largest Army
Largest Army

To claim Largest Army:

you must have played at least 3 knight cards
and have more played knights than anyone else

The reward:

2 victory points

If another player later exceeds your knight total, they take the card and the points.

Road Building

Effect:

place 2 free roads

Normal placement rules still apply. The roads must be legally placed as part of your network.

Year of Plenty

Effect:

take any 2 resource cards from the bank
they may be the same or different
Monopoly

Effect:

name one resource type
all other players must give you all resource cards of that type from their hands

Only cards in hand count, obviously.

Victory Point

Effect:

worth 1 victory point
kept hidden until needed or until game end

These are often things like chapel, library, market, etc. in themed editions, but mechanically they are just hidden VP cards.

Longest Road in detail

This is one of the trickier rules in Settlers.

Requirement to claim

To gain Longest Road, a player must have a continuous road of at least 5 road segments and be longer than every other player’s qualifying road.

Reward:

2 victory points
Important details
the road must be continuous
branching networks do not let you count all branches together; only one longest continuous path counts
another player’s settlement or city can break your road continuity if placed at a key intersection
your own settlements/cities do not break your road
Losing Longest Road

If someone else later creates a longer continuous road, they take the card and the 2 points.

This can create huge point swings.

Largest Army in detail
Requirement

You must have:

played at least 3 knights
more played knights than any other player

Reward:

2 victory points
Ties

A tie does not take the card away. Someone must exceed the current holder’s played knight count to take it.

Hidden and public information
Public information

Generally public:

board position
roads, settlements, cities
Longest Road / Largest Army status
number of development cards a player has bought, though not their types
visible point sources on the board
Hidden information

Usually hidden:

exact resource cards in players’ hands
exact types of unplayed development cards
hidden victory point development cards

This means players often estimate each other’s likely hands and point totals.

Score keeping

Settlers does not usually have a separate score track. Points are counted from what players have.

Public victory points

These are usually visible:

each settlement = 1
each city = 2
Longest Road = 2
Largest Army = 2
Hidden points

Victory point development cards are hidden.

That means a player who appears to have 8 visible points might actually have 9 or 10.

This creates an important tension:

players must watch for surprise wins
Winning timing

A player wins immediately when they reach 10 victory points on their own turn.

That matters.

Example:
If another player gives you Longest Road-breaking opportunity during their turn, you do not win right then unless the points are awarded on your turn? More precisely, card ownership and point totals can change immediately based on board state, but the official win condition is checked when a player reaches 10 on their turn. In normal gameplay, wins are announced on the active player’s turn when they build or reveal what gets them to 10.

A very common finish is:

build to 9 visible
reveal a hidden victory point for 10
or
take Longest Road / Largest Army for the final jump
Resource economy and probability

A major strategic feature of Settlers is the dice probability system.

Frequency of rolls

With two six-sided dice:

7 is most common
then 6 and 8
then 5 and 9
then 4 and 10
then 3 and 11
then 2 and 12

Since 7 triggers the robber instead of production, 6 and 8 are often the best production numbers.

Why placement matters

Your starting settlements determine your resource flow. Strong starting positions usually try to balance:

good numbers
useful resource mix
expansion routes
possible port access

A weak start can be hard to recover from unless trades and development cards go very well.

Starting placement strategy basics

The setup phase is arguably the most important part of the game.

Players usually consider:

Resource diversity

Can you access several different resources early?

If you miss an important resource entirely, you may become too dependent on trade.

Strong numbers

Does your starting position touch good numbers like 6, 8, 5, or 9?

Expansion options

Can your roads grow toward open settlement spots?

Ore-grain-wool vs brick-lumber balance

Different styles exist:

brick/lumber supports rapid road + settlement expansion
ore/grain supports cities and development cards
wool is often less individually powerful but is needed for settlements and development cards
Port plans

A port can compensate for weak resource access if you can reliably produce that resource.

Resource shortages and the “table economy”

Settlers is interesting because the value of a resource changes depending on:

the map
the number tokens
who has access to what
current player needs

Example:

if brick is scarce on the board, 1 brick can become very valuable
if everyone has wool, wool may become cheap in trades

This creates a player-driven economy.

Hand management

Because rolling a 7 can force discards, holding too many cards is risky.

This means players constantly weigh:

keep saving for a city
or spend now to avoid losing half the hand?

Hand management is one of the subtle but important parts of the game.

Blocking and player interaction

Settlers is not purely about building your own thing. It includes indirect conflict.

Robber pressure

You can slow opponents by parking the robber on their best hex.

Road races

Players compete to reach limited settlement spots first.

Settlement blocking

Because of the distance rule, placing a settlement can deny nearby intersections to others.

Breaking Longest Road

A well-placed settlement can cut an opponent’s continuous road line.

Trade politics

Players often decide not to help the leader by refusing trades.

A lot of Settlers skill is not just tactical, but social.

Important rule limits
Building supply

You can only build if you still have the piece available in your personal supply.

If you run out of settlements, you cannot build more settlements until one is upgraded to a city and returned to your supply.

If you run out of roads, you cannot build more roads.

Bank resource limits

If the bank does not have enough of a resource to pay everyone entitled to receive it, then by standard rules nobody gets that resource from that production.

This is rare but can happen.

Development card deck limits

Once the deck is empty, no more development cards can be bought.

Turn flexibility and action flow

A common beginner question is whether all building must happen after all trading. In practice, after the dice roll, the active player can usually manage trade and build actions flexibly.

For example:

trade with a player
build a road
make a maritime trade
buy a development card
build a settlement

as long as each action is legal and all costs are paid.

The main fixed part is:

the dice roll comes first
then normal action/trading/building happens
What you cannot do

Some important prohibitions:

you cannot build a settlement adjacent to another settlement/city
you cannot place disconnected roads
you cannot build a settlement not connected to your road network after setup
you cannot upgrade directly to a city on an empty spot
you usually cannot play a development card the same turn you bought it
you cannot trade development cards
you cannot use another player’s roads as your own connection
other players cannot trade with each other on your turn unless you are part of the trade
you cannot move the robber unless a 7 is rolled or a knight is played
Typical flow of a game

A full game usually unfolds like this:

Early game
players place initial settlements
resource production begins
players race for first expansion spots
brick and lumber are often very valuable
the board starts to get claimed
Mid game
more settlements appear
cities begin to matter
ports may come online
robber pressure increases
Longest Road and Largest Army become serious objectives
trades become more political
Late game
available expansion spots shrink
cities and development cards become very strong
players track visible and hidden points closely
table often gangs up on the leader through robber placement and trade refusal
surprise wins from hidden VP cards become common
Common paths to victory

There is no single required strategy, but common winning styles include:

Expansion strategy

Focus on:

roads
settlements
good board coverage
maybe Longest Road

This often relies on strong brick/lumber access early.

City strategy

Focus on:

ore
grain
upgrading quickly to cities
generating high-value production
Development card strategy

Focus on:

ore, grain, wool
buying many development cards
pushing for Largest Army
sometimes winning with hidden VP cards
Port engine strategy

Focus on:

dominating one resource
using a 2:1 or 3:1 port to convert efficiently

Good players often blend these rather than commit purely to one.

The role of luck vs skill

Settlers includes both.

Luck
dice rolls
development card draws
robber timing
table trade opportunities
Skill
starting placements
probability evaluation
trade negotiation
timing
hand management
road planning
reading opponents’ likely plans
knowing when to race, block, or pivot

Over one game, luck can matter a lot. Over many games, skill matters more.

Features that define Settlers
1. Shared production system

Everyone can gain resources on any player’s turn.

This keeps players engaged even when it is not their turn.

2. Negotiated trading

This is one of the game’s signature features. It makes the experience much more social than many strategy games.

3. Modular board

The terrain and numbers can be randomized, giving replay value.

4. Tactical blocking without combat armies

There is interaction and aggression, but not through direct unit battles.

5. Hidden point tension

Because victory point development cards are hidden, exact standings can be uncertain.

6. Spatial network building

Road placement and settlement spacing create meaningful positional play.

Beginner-friendly summary of the rules

If you wanted the whole game boiled down simply:

Build the island and place number tokens
Each player places 2 starting settlements and 2 roads
Collect starting resources from your second settlement
On your turn, roll the dice
Matching hexes produce resources for players with adjacent settlements/cities
If a 7 is rolled, players with more than 7 cards discard half, then the robber moves and steals
Trade with players or with the bank
Spend resources to build roads, settlements, cities, or buy development cards
Settlements must connect to your roads and cannot be adjacent to another building
Cities replace settlements and produce double
Longest Road and Largest Army each give 2 points
First player to reach 10 victory points wins
